~ About Me ~





The name's Zheng. Daisy Zheng.


I'm currently a senior attending Cornell University, set to graduate in May 2018 with a B.S. in Computer Science and a minor in Biological Sciences. Outside of my professional and academic pursuits, I'm also an avid photographer, athlete, artist, writer, and guitarist.

I write code that visualizes the world.


Whether it's visual effects, images, data, or fantastical landscapes, I like to write programs that visually make sense of some aspect of our world or imagination. So far, this has fallen under 4 main categories:

  • Graphics
  • Computer Vision
  • Game Development
  • Data Visualization
I've worked on projects across the entire software stack, using languages including:
  • Python, Java, OCaml, C, JavaScript, and HTML/CSS
and utilizing libraries such as:
  • WebGL, Three.js, D3, NumPy, OpenCV, and Scikit-Image.

I'll let my experience speak for itself.


Professionally speaking, I've spent my last 2 summers interning at companies working on computer vision projects. My first internship, at the startup Infinity ProAV, involved implementing and optimizing a 3D stereo reconstruction algorithm that could be used in real-time vehicle detection. Lectures and school projects can really only take you so far: this internship gave me the chance to test my mettle against a real business need. Though there was a lot for me to learn, this is one thing I'm willing to take pride it: I will do whatever it takes, and learn whatever I need to, in order to accomplish my goals.

My next internship, this time at the biotechnology corporation Biogen , I had the unique opportunity to tackle a problem that's continued to plague even the biggest biotech players out there. Though it would be downright arrogant to claim that my solution was perfect when professional companies have pooled all their resources on trying to develop their own solutions, it was complete and most importantly, it worked. I had successfully built a program from scratch that could analyze 3D neuronal microscopy scans, identify the individual voxels corresponding to different neuron structures, and visualize the results. For Biogen, it was the proof that they needed in order to conclude that building a 3D neuron tracer like I had done was feasible and worth pursuing. For me, it was proof that I have what it takes to take on a challenge and find a way to solve it.

My life has shaped my story, even if it does not define it.


I grew up with computers. Given the generation I grew up in, I suppose that's hardly surprising. Still, it plays an important role in the direction my life took. For a time, it probably seemed like I would stray towards the ECE side of things, given my propensity for "playing" with my parents' soldering tools. In the end, it was my fascination with game development and my dream of entering the animation industry that propelled me down a different path, a path that led me towards computer science.


Still, some passions you just can't leave behind. I'm a person with many, sometimes conflicting, passions. I had long harbored a love of biology, a love that hasn't truly lessened since the day in 3rd grade when I declared that I wanted to be a marine biologist and study dolphins. Though I had made the decision to major in Computer Science when I came to Cornell, I knew that I would also be taking my share of biology courses, so it only made sense to take the Biology minor as well.


If there's one thing I learned as an athlete, it's that life takes determination. Gymnastics takes a lot of things - strength, grit, hard work, mental fortitude... Most of all, though, it takes determination. Growing up as a competitive gymnast who dreamed of going to the Olympics, I learned this lesson well. We had a saying at my gym: "Eat like a champion, sleep like a champion, live like a champion.". To be the best, it's not enough to be just a punch-clock gymnast (or developer, or artist, or anything else). To be the best, you have to live and breathe your passions, you have to be willing to do whatever it takes, whenever it takes, for however long it takes. This is a lesson I've carried with me through my whole life, and it's one I'll never forget.


This drive to explore: I'll never let it go. Explorations. Expeditions. New frontiers. These are things that have always fascinated me. For me, it started with just exploring my neighborhood or the back woods. Then it moved on to the country. Then it expanded to the entire world. Of course, my definition of exploration isn't limited to physical lands. There are frontiers in science and technology, art, knowledge, and any other number of imaginable fields just waiting to be discovered, and that idea excites me more than anything. This is part of the reason why I love computer science and biology so much: they are two of the most rapidly evolving fields existing at this moment, with so many areas just waiting to be explored.

Oh, and did I mention that I'm a photographer, too?


I was originally inspired to seriously start photography back in 2011. What started simply as a way to document my summer quickly grew into a love of capturing those fleeting moments in life that ordinarily would just pass us by.

Along my journey, I’ve encountered a lot of conflicting philosophies on just what it means to be a photographer. Some saw photography as art, others saw it as truth. It's made me think a lot about where I stand, but all I really know right now is that I believe in capturing how I see the world. Whether that’s the ‘truth’ or not, well that’s up for debate. But whatever it is, it’s my truth. I never set out to be a photojournalist, and I didn’t set out to be an artist either. All I wanted was to remember the moment, to capture those stories and those emotions and all the countless other things that make up that little piece of history. And that’s still what I want, and it will continue to be what I want. That much I know for sure.
